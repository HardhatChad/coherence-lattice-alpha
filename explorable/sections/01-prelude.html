PreludeWhat it means for a lattice to be alive

Here are two little oscillators, connected by a spring. The spring's stiffness is called its coupling, written K. In ordinary physics, K is a number you choose and then leave alone.

This is the setup physics has used for a century. Fix K, pick initial phases, watch them evolve. If K is large enough, they synchronize. If not, they drift.

Now try something different. Instead of holding K fixed, let it evolve too. Give it its own equation of motion. Tell it:

grow when the phases you connect tend to align  ·  shrink when they fight

That is, roughly, the Coherence Learning Rule. The coupling itself becomes a participant in the dynamics. A living variable, not a dial.

Nothing dramatic yet. Two oscillators are too small a world. But watch what happens when you scale this up to a network — thousands of oscillators, each pair with its own living K, all governed by the same local rule. Bonds find their equilibria. Some couplings converge to a large value (alive bonds), others decay to zero (dead bonds). The network self-organizes into a binary field of connectivity, from any starting point.

Pick the network carefully — we will see later why the diamond lattice is mathematically forced upon us — and something remarkable happens. Topological defects appear out of nothing. They carry quantized charge. They have spin 1/2. They are confined by a logarithmic potential. Their magnetic moment is g = 2 at tree level. And the coupling constant that governs how they interact with themselves is

1/α = 137.035999

— to 1.5 parts per billion of the measured value, with zero free parameters.

This is the story. It takes fourteen short chapters to tell. We start with one bond.
